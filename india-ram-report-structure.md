## Report Structure (same 8-stage RAM framework as Saudi)

### Chapter 1: Diagnosis of the National AI Landscape (p.6)
- Quasi-federal structure (Union/State/Concurrent lists)
- IndiaAI Mission, NITI Aayog history
- PSA AI Committee → AI Governance Guidelines (Nov 2025)
- India as "AI garage for the world"

### Chapter 2: Legal and Regulatory Dimension (p.12)
- AI policy evolution: 2018 NITI Aayog → 2025 AI Governance Guidelines
- IndiaAI Mission (₹10,372 crore / ~$1.25B)
- Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023
- IT Act intermediary guidelines, deepfakes regulation
- RTI Act, procurement frameworks

### Chapter 3: Social and Cultural Dimension (p.26)
- Diversity in STEM, gender gaps
- Indigenous language training (Bhashini)
- Public engagement and trust
- AI in health (ICMR guidelines, CoWIN, Ayushman Bharat)
- AI for accessibility, children's health
- Cultural heritage

### Chapter 4: Scientific and Educational Dimension (p.35)
- AI research publications growth
- IndiaAI FutureSkills, Centres of Excellence
- School curricula (CBSE AI courses from Class 8)
- Higher education: 400+ AI/ML programs
- NPTEL, SWAYAM MOOCs

### Chapter 5: Economic Dimension (p.41)
- Labour markets, talent concentration
- AI skilling landscape
- Start-ups (3,000+ AI start-ups)
- Private investment growth
- Intermediate consumption patterns

### Chapter 6: Technical and Infrastructural Dimension (p.49)
- Digital public infrastructure (Aadhaar, UPI, DigiLocker)
- IndiaAI Compute: 10,000 GPUs planned
- Applied standards (BIS, TEC AI robustness)
- Cybersecurity (ITU GCI ranking)

### Chapter 7: (Not explicitly numbered — covered in Ch.8)

### Chapter 8: Findings from Multistakeholder Consultations (p.56)
4 themes:
1. AI Governance and Policy
2. AI Adoption: Enablers and Barriers
3. Preparing India's Workforce for AI
4. Infrastructure and Investment

### Chapter 9: Policy Recommendations (p.62)
8 recommendations:

| # | Recommendation | Key Focus |
|---|---------------|-----------|
| 1 | Ensure informed AI governance through evidence-based risk and legal review | Sector-specific risk assessment, regulatory adequacy |
| 2 | Co-develop guidelines for operationalizing ethical AI principles | Practical tools for developers/deployers |
| 3 | Strengthen centre–state coordination for AI governance | Joint task forces, state-level capacity |
| 4 | Leverage AIKosh platform for high-quality datasets | Data offices, certified data officers |
| 5 | Advance public trust through governance, procurement and awareness | Transparency, AISI role, procurement standards |
| 6 | Prepare workforce for AI future | Skilling, ethics embedding, inclusive transitions |
| 7 | Integrate environmental sustainability into AI infrastructure | Environmental cost study, energy-efficient models |
| 8 | Targeted interventions for diversity in AI workforce & R&D | Under-represented groups, regional language modules |

## Key Differences from Saudi Report
- **8 recommendations** (vs Saudi's 21) — broader/higher-level
- **No explicit 1-5 dimension scores in PDF** (scores on UNESCO website)
- **Stronger focus on:** federalism/centre-state coordination, digital public infra (DPI model), language diversity (22 official languages)
- **India-specific:** IndiaAI Mission, Bhashini, NITI Aayog, Aadhaar/UPI ecosystem
- **122 pages** vs Saudi's 87 — significantly more detailed
